Resilience in Society: Addressing Disaster Risk Reduction through Sustainable Framework Development
"Disaster risk reduction is the concept and practice of reducing disaster risks through systematic efforts to analyze and reduce the causal factors of disasters."
Throughout the past few years, the international community has faced many disasters: natural disasters brought on by climate change, the COVID pandemic, and more conflicts that are results of human actions. The global society is susceptible to disruption at any moment. Even though the pandemic and other disasters may be quietly fading away, it is certain that the immense damage it has caused to global agendas such as the Sustainable Development Goals and the Paris Agreement will remain for quite some time to come. This further reinforced the necessity of resilience in the face of such disasters.
A Disaster Risk Reduction plan like the Sendai Framework provided tangible actions not only to curb losses, but also to ensure our development for the future. It is imperative that key actors such as strengthening disaster risk governance, building capacity, establishing research institutions, sharing information, and providing policy advice play a major role in preventing systematic risk and promoting resilience within society. Our goal is to build resilience rather than passively react to unpredictable disasters by establishing durable and sustainable frameworks in areas such as education, humanitarian assistance and more.

Issue (101): Discerning the balance between nuclear energy proliferation and nuclear weaponization
Nuclear energy, though not renewable, is much more sustainable than other forms of energy proliferation, such as the burning of fossil fuels. This is because a large majority of the materials used in this proliferation are recyclable, with up to 96% of it being reusable. This makes it a preferable alternative for LEDCs, as though the plants are expensive to construct, they're relatively inexpensive to run. Therefore, nuclear energy has high potential as the primary energy source for underdeveloped countries.
However, the harnessing of nuclear energy itself accompanies a much higher risk: nuclear weaponization. There have been extensive international issues centered around the ambiguous balance between nuclear energy development and nuke production, such as the Iran Nuclear Act and the US's withdrawal from the pact. Now, many countries use nuclear weapons to hold a strategic advantage and deterrence over other nations.
The issue spans over virtually every country because member states are either concerned about energy usage or weaponization.

Co-Chair: Shannon Yen
Issue (102): Preventing illicit weapons from being distributed to non-state actors causing terrorism
In a world of rising conflicts and political turmoil, the trafficking of illicit weapons, often known as “arms trafficking,” are causes of mass destruction against others, and have been of great concern to member states around the world. The question is: How will these illicit weapons be detected, and if so, how can actions be taken to ensure that they do not land in the wrong hands? Delegates should appropriately consider the reasons behind why these illicit weapons are even able to land in the hands of terrorists in the first place.
Governmental entities, as well as other organizations, such as private arms dealers, who have the funds and means to transport these illicit weapons, take great measures to cover their actions against the possibility of rebuke by the global community. Therefore, the issue at hand will be somewhat challenging in the realm of determining what regulations and preventative measures will truly solve the issue at hand, given the dominance of Western-made weapons in the arms trafficking scene but the common prevalence of these weapons in areas of high terrorist activity, such as in areas of Africa where governmental control is lacking. To summarize, combating illicit weapons is a tricky topic, one where sneakiness is common and circumvention is key.

Head Chair: Melvin Huang
Issue (201): Promotion of Resilient Framework for Equitable and Accessible Food Distribution
Amid the crises that arose in the pandemic era, food insecurity amounted to one of the most imperative issues to be addressed worldwide. The breakdown of the global supply chain, significant diminution of agricultural production export in Ukraine, rising crude oil prices, abnormal climate events, and increasingly prevalent civil unrest all culminate in the uneven distribution of sustenance as well as unaffordable food prices, which in turn leads to record high malnutrition and famine across continents. Consequently, while the World Trade Organization held its 12th Ministerial Conference in June 2022, high-level ministers of member states delivered two substantive outcomes thereof: World Food Programme exemption from export restriction as well as the Declaration on the Emergency Response to Food Insecurity, which purported to “ensure that any emergence measures introduced to address food security concerns shall minimize trade distortions as far as possible”. But were those sufficient? More and more member states are calling for not only mere promises and emergency aid but also long-term institutions for resolving future food insecurity issues. Thus, therein lies the core question: Is there a way to build a long-term framework to maintain food security? And if so, how?

Co-Chair: Judy Li
Issue (202): Facilitating Trade in Medical Goods in the Context of Covid-19
The COVID-19 pandemic has had a significant impact on the global economy and international trade, bringing unprecedented disruptions to people's lives. The pandemic led to rising demand for medical goods trading, such as vaccines, face masks, and other critical pandemic products. Despite the decline in global trade, the market for medical goods registered an average growth rate of more than 14% from 2020 and 2021.
However, there are also several issues relevant to the global trade of medical supplies. For instance, insufficient supply of medical products has caused unequal distribution and increased prices. Export restrictions were posed, delaying essential transactions of critical medical products and further exacerbating supply insecurity. In particular, driven by overwhelming demands and inadequate supply, the prices of face masks were raised to an exorbitantly high level. Furthermore, the world is primarily dependent on certain countries to provide critical pandemic products, resulting in limited participation of underdeveloped countries and possible infringements by more dominant countries upon their economy and politics. Thus, the unequal distribution of medical goods aggravated the difficulties of the economy while also obstructing the recovery of underdeveloped countries from the pandemic. The WTO has since urged countries to roll back export restrictions and put trade-facilitating measures in place.

Issue (301): Preserving diversity in domestic industries amidst foreign corporate monopoly
As globalization continues to fuel economic expansion in developed and developing countries, the predominantly laissez-faire trading system led to the rise of certain global industry mongrels that monopolize markets and depower small businesses of local origins. Though most governments have innate protection mechanisms to avoid total reliance on foreign businesses, the lack of sufficient regulations on international trade organization and even profiting countries themselves has sustained a creeping of neoimperialism in exploited countries.
With the 10th Sustainable Development Goals named "Reduced Inequalities," it's often that campaigns focus on income and social recognition as the main targets of the issue. Yet in view of these goals being "sustainable" for development, countries must also allocate resources in ensuring equality to wage business to help people maintain their own living. While this issue got some spotlight in chapter 3 of UNDP's 2021 Regional Human Development Report for Latin America and the Caribbean, the phenomenon of foreign monopoly persists in many other regions of the world. Aside from the common methods of tariffs and per-unit taxation, there must also be efforts on promoting domestic businesses to level the playing field in a market economy. Member states ought to devise policies balanced between freedom of privatization and freedom to compete.

Deputy President: Lucas Lin
Issue (302): Enhancing Household Resilience to Poverty and Vulnerability in Developing Countries
The outbreak of the Covid-19 pandemic has permanently increased poverty vulnerability among households in developing countries, undoing a decade of UN efforts toward poverty reduction. The notion of vulnerability includes efforts of households to cope with fluctuations and shocks, as well as means to reduce risks of falling into poverty. These idiosyncratic shocks ranging from health emergencies, social hardships, rising costs of foodstuff and living needs require policy concern from national governments as well as through the UNDP disaster risk reduction framework to enhance preparedness and resilience. Members of poor households are more vulnerable to illness and financially incapable of affording medical attention while others may suffer from job and income loss and fail to support their families and communities. Countries such as Cambodia, El Salvador, and South Africa have endured the socioeconomic disruptions caused by the pandemic, meaning that safety nets, poverty alleviation programs, and financial inclusion need to be strengthened to support poverty-stricken households to cope with risks. Vulnerability and poverty is a multidimensional issue that considers health, education, nutrition, and standard of living beyond just the measurement of income. By assessing vulnerability through UNDP's multidimensional vulnerability indices (MVIs), both the public and private sector can inform policy alternatives through a more inclusive framework that addresses the impacts of health uncertainties, social strife and poor infrastructure in tackling global poverty.

Issue (401): Sino-Indian Border Dispute
China and India's border dispute is renownedly agitated in Arunachal Pradesh amidst a stretch of over 3,440 kilometers. A region to the northeast of India, Arunachal Pradesh's state annexation in 1987 and Indian leaders' proceeding visits had long irked China. Fatal conflicts involving sticks since June 2020 - eventually gunshots in December 2022 - escalated the unrest despite how a 1996 agreement prohibited firearms in the region. Although both sides disengaged, tension accumulated as they scrambled to build infrastructure along the border.
This conflict holds implications in the environmental sphere. The Himalayan plateau's monsoonal rains and seasonal ice-melts fed rivers to South, Southeast and East Asia; however, toxic leaks from both nuclear powers' mines and agricultural developments loom over the water source. Construction of roads and hospitals that induce deforestation can also compromise the Himalayas' third-largest biodiversity hotspot. On the humanitarian side, water supplies regulated by hydroelectric plants and dams threaten the livelihood of up to 630 million people, with Tibetans and other minorities calling for autonomy between the two parties' intensifying grip.
Overall, dynamic politics complicate the environmental and humanitarian issues, with both the Chinese and Indian government seeking allies that may result in further stalemates and prolong the dispute.

Deputy President: Kingston Yu
Issue (402): Responses to the Insurgency in the Maghreb
The Insurgency in the Maghreb refers to the violent clash between governments in the Maghreb-Sahel region in Africa and the Islamic militants that started since the Algerian Civil War in 2002. Such violent groups include Al-Qaeda and the Islamic State (ISIL).
The fact that extremist beliefs continue to define and characterize the wave of insurgencies that has plagued the Maghreb Sahel region for so long raises some troubling considerations. After violent jihadists brought Algeria's insurgency in the 1990s into infamy, people expect that dissident rebels would stop adopting extreme ideology since it alienates the majority of local communities, fragments the ranks of rebels, and scares away outside supporters. Seeing such risks, it is perplexing why Salafi jihadism continues to be the insurgent repertoire in the Maghreb-Sahel region. What's more puzzling is that this radical ideology has evolved into the ideal weapon of war and the ideological hub that unites the support of various types of oppressed communities. Salafi jihadism aided Sahel rebels to survive, recruit, and outcompete other contesting armed players. As such, it is of great importance to the Security Council in resolving the bloodshed and restoring peace and order to the region.
